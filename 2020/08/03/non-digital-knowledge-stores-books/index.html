non-digital knowledge stores (books)

2020-08-03

It may be a bit square to refer to keep a library of books in this day and age, but I find books to be reassuringly apocalypse-resistant repositories of knowledge, often with far clearer and more understandable explanations than you’ll find on most statistics and data science web pages. Here’s a small sample of my statistics books:

  • “Applied Statistics: Principles and Examples” by D.R.Cox and E.J.Snell (Chapman & Hall, 1981)
  • “Problem Solving: A Statistician’s Guide” by C.Chatfield (Chapman & Hall, 1988)
  • “An Introduction To Non-Parametric Methods” by P.Sprent (Penguin, 1981)
  • “The Art of Statistics” by Prof. D.Spiegelhalter (Pelican, 2019)
  • “Fundamentals of Bio-statistics” by B.Rosner (Brooks/Cole, 2000)
  • “R for Data Science” by H.Wickham and G.Grolemund (O’Reilly, 2016)

Despite my grumblings above, there are some absolutely awesome texts online, including “R for Data Science” above, and:

“Elements of Statistical Learning” by Hastie, Tibshirani, Friedman</</e

“Forecasting: Principles & Practice” by R.Hyndman

“Reinforcement Learning: An Introduction” by R.Sutton and A.Barto
