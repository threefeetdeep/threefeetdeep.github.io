Am I being interfered with?

2020-08-22

Not a reference to sexual abuse, just an enticing article name to lure you in…

This article is inspired by this BBC new article about a village losing their broadband due to an old, and very electrically noisy TV set…

I’ve spent much of my working life as a practicing RF engineer, and know firsthand just how challenging it can be to troubleshoot these kind of “EMC”, or electromagnetic compatibility problems.

99.9% of us don’t have the knowledge or access to very expensive test equipment like spectrum or signal analyzers to be able to diagnose these kind of issues. The problem is, they do crop up in everyday life!

Examples

  • Your kitchen radio sound becomes “buzzy” or won’t even tune in anymore
  • When listening to music on your expensive hi-fi, an annoying clicking sound start to spoil your enjoyment.
  • Watching TV, you notice that you can’t tune into many of the channels you watched yesterday.
  • Your Wi-Fi was working great, but one day your download speed plummets, and never recovers.

All of the above can be caused by radio frequency interference.

Pointers

In case you suffer from such an interference problem at home, here are a few pointers to hopefully help you find the cause. It’s also worth bearing in mind that ocassionally, you might be the source of the problem, and not the victim!

Spot the patterns

It’s a good idea to spend some effort to record things like:

  • When did the problem start happening (time/date)?
  • When does the problem occur? Every morning at 7am? All day? Only some evenings?
  • Does it affect more than one of your devices? You PC and your kitchen radio? Just your TV?
  • Does it affect other similar devices, e.g. all Wi-Fi devices in your home? Any TV you have?

Tricks to find the cause

There are a few common “culprits”, or sources of interference in a domestic setting.

  • Power-line “Ethernet” (using the AC wiring in a house as a “network” cable)
  • A neighbour changes the type, location or settings of their Wi-Fi router
  • Damaged cables, e.g. TV aerial cabling damaged by mice, age, weathering etc.
  • A mobile phone basestation has been erected nearby.

Be nosy, ask the neighbours if they have got a new, snazzy device like a new router, or power-line Ethernet connection. Or get up in your loft to inspect your TV or satellite aerial cabling.

If you have a portable kitchen radio, especially one with “analogue AM/FM, it can be used as a primitive “radio frequency direction finder” if you can hear the interference using the radio - put simply, you walk around the house, trying to find where the noise of the interference is loudest.
